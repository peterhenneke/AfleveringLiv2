\section*{Exercise (iv)}

We argue that $V^0(t,x) = x$ by verification: we show that $V^0(t,x) = x$ satisfies
Thiele's partial differential equation (PDE) in both phases with the correct terminal
boundary condition, and conclude by uniqueness.

\subsection*{Thiele's PDE}

The market reserve $V^0(t,x)$ is defined as the expected discounted future net outflows,
\[
  V^0(t,x) = \mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{P}\otimes\mathbb{Q}}\!\left[
    \int_t^{111} e^{-\int_t^s (\tilde{r}(v)+\mu(v))\,dv}\, c(s,X(s))\,ds
    \;\Big|\; X(t)=x,Z(t)=0
  \right].
\]
By the Feynman--Kac theorem---equivalently, by applying It\^{o}'s formula to the
discounted process $e^{-\int_0^t(\tilde{r}+\mu)\,ds}V^0(t,X(t))$ and requiring its
drift to equal the outflow rate---$V^0(t,x)$ satisfies Thiele's PDE
\begin{equation}\label{eq:thiele_pde}
  \frac{\partial V^0}{\partial t}
  + a(t,x)\,\frac{\partial V^0}{\partial x}
  + \tfrac{1}{2}\,b(t,x)^2\,\frac{\partial^2 V^0}{\partial x^2}
  = \bigl(\tilde{r}(t)+\mu(t)\bigr)V^0 - c(t,x),
\end{equation}
where $a(t,x)$ and $b(t,x)$ are the drift and diffusion of $X$, and $c(t,x)$ is
the net outflow rate (benefits minus premiums).

\subsection*{Derivatives of $V^0(t,x) = x$}

We conjecture $V^0(t,x) = x$ and compute its partial derivatives:
\[
  \frac{\partial V^0}{\partial t} = 0,
  \qquad
  \frac{\partial V^0}{\partial x} = 1,
  \qquad
  \frac{\partial^2 V^0}{\partial x^2} = 0.
\]
Substituting into~\eqref{eq:thiele_pde}, the PDE reduces to verifying
\begin{equation}\label{eq:thiele_reduced}
  a(t,x) = \bigl(\tilde{r}(t)+\mu(t)\bigr)x - c(t,x).
\end{equation}

\subsection*{Retirement phase, $t \in [m, 111]$}

From SDE~(2), the drift coefficient is
\[
  a(t,x) = \bigl(\tilde{r}(t)+\mu(t)\bigr)x - \frac{x}{\tilde{V}^0(t)},
\]
There is no death benefit during retirement (the account is released to the
pool on death), so the net outflow rate consists solely of the annuity payout,
$c(t,x)=x/\tilde{V}^0(t)$.

We need to verify~\eqref{eq:thiele_reduced}, i.e.\ that
$a(t,x) = \bigl(\tilde{r}+\mu\bigr)x - c(t,x)$.
Equivalently, we show that the left-hand side minus the right-hand side
is zero:
\begin{align*}
  &a(t,x) - \bigl(\tilde{r}+\mu\bigr)x + c(t,x) \\[4pt]
  %% Step 1: insert expressions for a and c
  &= \Bigl[\bigl(\tilde{r}+\mu\bigr)x
     - \frac{x}{\tilde{V}^0(t)}\Bigr]
     - \bigl(\tilde{r}+\mu\bigr)x
     + \frac{x}{\tilde{V}^0(t)} \\[4pt]
  &= 0. \quad\checkmark
\end{align*}

\subsection*{Active phase, $t \in [30, m)$}

From SDE~(1), the drift is
\[
  a(t,x) = \tilde{r}(t)\,x + \theta L(t) - \mu(t)\bigl(KL(t)-x\bigr)
\]
During the active phase the policyholder pays premiums at rate $\theta L(t)$
(an inflow to the insurer) and the insurer pays death benefits at expected
rate $\mu(t)\,KL(t)$.
The net outflow rate is therefore
\[
  c(t,x) = \mu(t)\,KL(t) - \theta L(t).
\]

We verify~\eqref{eq:thiele_reduced} in the same way as above:
\begin{align*}
  &a(t,x) - \bigl(\tilde{r}+\mu\bigr)x + c(t,x) \\[4pt]
  %% Step 1: insert expressions for a and c
  &= \bigl[(\tilde{r}+\mu)x + \theta L - \mu KL\bigr]
     - (\tilde{r}+\mu)x
     + \bigl[\mu KL - \theta L\bigr] \\[4pt]
  &= 0. \quad\checkmark
\end{align*}

\subsection*{Boundary condition}

At the terminal age $t=111$ there are no future obligations, so the
boundary condition is $V^0(111,x) = 0$.
If $V^0(t,x) = x$, this requires $X(111) = 0$.
This is guaranteed by construction of the calculation basis:
the reserve $\tilde{V}^0(t)$ computed in Exercise~(i) satisfies
$\tilde{V}^0(111)=0$, so the annuity payout rate
$X(t)/\tilde{V}^0(t) \to \infty$ as $t\to 111$, which drains the
account completely.
In other words, the purpose of $\tilde{V}^0$ is precisely to calibrate
the payout rate so that the account is exhausted at the maximum age.
The simulation from Exercise~(ii) confirms this numerically:
$X(111)\approx 0$.

\subsection*{Conclusion}

Since $V^0(t,x)=x$ satisfies Thiele's PDE~\eqref{eq:thiele_pde} in both phases and
matches the terminal boundary condition, uniqueness of the solution gives
\[
  \boxed{V^0(t,x) = x.}
\]
The market reserve equals the account value. This is the defining property of a
unit-link product: the policyholder bears all investment risk, and the account
dynamics are constructed to continuously budget for every mortality and benefit cash
flow, making the account the exact self-financing replicating portfolio for all
future obligations.
